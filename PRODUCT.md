# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

The primary reader is a Pittsburgh small business owner who finds the
publication through a creator post, a search, or word of mouth, sees
their own record, and lands here in evaluation mode. They are not a
designer or a marketer. They have a business to run and a thin slice
of attention for tools that promise to help.

The ultimate goal of the publication is to move that owner into a
free Relay trial. The publication is the funnel. Every page should
treat the owner as the highest-value reader, even when the surface
is being read by someone else.

Secondary reader: Pittsburgh creators (TikTok and Instagram) sourcing
under-covered local places to film. They scan rank, photo count, and
the reviews to decide whether a record is worth a visit.

Tertiary reader: curious locals discovering the publication. They
land on a record, scroll editorial, follow links, share. Their
behavior signals what's resonating.

## Product Purpose

The Burgh Quarterly is a quarterly editorial publication that ranks
Pittsburgh small businesses by creator-readiness signals (review
depth and sentiment, photo catalog, conversion path, Instagram
cadence, creator fit). It is published by Relay (run-relay.com), a
vetted local micro-influencer platform.

The publication exists for two reasons. First, to be a property
Pittsburghers actually want to read every quarter, distinct from
Yelp and from generic listicles, with a strong editorial voice and
real point of view. Second, to convert the small business owners
reading their own records into Relay free-trial users by making the
gap between where they sit and where they could sit visible,
specific, and addressable.

Success looks like:
- The publication is something Pittsburghers anticipate every season.
- Every owner who reads their own record can name one specific move
  the page tells them to make.
- A measurable share of those owners click through to a Relay free
  trial after reading.
- Creators use the publication as a sourcing list before Relay even
  reaches out.

## Brand Personality

**Sharp. Confident. Generous.**

Sharp: takes positions, names specific things. No "best of" hedging.
Confident: doesn't apologize for the rankings, doesn't bury its
methodology in caveats. The numbers are what they are.
Generous: low-ranked businesses are framed as underrated, not weak.
Every record surfaces at least one dimension the business is doing
well. The publication celebrates the long tail rather than shaming
it.

Voice anchors: New York Times Upshot for data-driven editorial,
Eater for local-restaurants-with-attitude tone, Pitchfork for
opinion-first ranking energy.

## Anti-references

Explicitly NOT:

- Yelp. Algorithmic-feeling user reviews, no editorial voice, no
  point of view.
- Listicles ("The 10 Best Cafes in Pittsburgh"). Generic, SEO-shaped,
  voiceless.
- SEO-farmed local blogs. Programmatic content farms that publish
  by template.
- Marketing-tool screenshots. Burgh Quarterly is editorial, not a
  SaaS dashboard.

Forbidden language (from EDITORIAL_VOICE.md, kept here for register):

- "leverage creator partnerships," "amplify your brand"
- "content strategy," "organic growth," "authentic engagement"
- "best bakery," "top-rated," letter grades, numeric composite scores
- yinzer dialect (yinz, dahntahn, jagoff, Primanti's clichés)
- "we noticed your X could be stronger" (patronizing, surveillance-flavored)
- "unlock your rankings" (gatewall energy)
- the word "median" in user-facing copy (use "family typical" instead)

## Strategic Principles

1. **Editorial all the way through.** Every section is curated. The
   former "loud editorial / quiet record" architecture (records as
   Wikipedia-neutral) is retired in favor of editorial energy on
   every page, including business records. The opinion lives
   everywhere; the verdict is the brand.

2. **Owner as the highest-value reader.** When a design call is
   ambiguous between owner-clarity and creator-elegance, owner
   wins. The page must answer the owner's "where do I sit and what
   do I do about it" instantly.

3. **Distance to next tier, never failure.** Low-ranked businesses
   are underrated, not weak. Every record names at least one signal
   the business is doing well. Movement framing beats absolute
   position.

4. **Sub-category peer pools, not full-family lumping.** An Italian
   restaurant compares to other Italian restaurants. A vintage
   boutique to other vintage boutiques. The "Pittsburgh Restaurants
   600+" pool is too coarse to feel meaningful.

5. **Numbers behind opinions, not in front of them.** The page never
   surfaces a 0-100 composite score. Tier badges, rank-of-N, and
   "family typical" framing carry the weight. The math is
   underneath, not on top.

6. **Relay appears in two places only.** The masthead colophon and
   one sidebar CTA on claimed business pages. Never named in
   articles. Conversion comes from the publication being good
   enough that owners ask, "who's behind this?"

7. **No numeric scores publicly.** Composites, subscore numbers,
   percentile points. None of it. Bars encode visually. Rank
   replaces score. Letter grades are forbidden.

## Accessibility

WCAG AA across the board.

- Body text and editorial blocks at 4.5:1 contrast minimum.
- Every animation respects `prefers-reduced-motion` with a fallback
  that does not lose information.
- All interactive elements keyboard-navigable, with visible focus
  states.
- Screen-reader semantics on rank labels, tier badges, and the
  `aria-current` self-row in scoreboards.
- Tap targets at least 44x44px.
